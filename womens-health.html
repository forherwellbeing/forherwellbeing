What is PCOS?

PCOS affects 6–15% of women globally, presenting differently in every woman. Some have primarily metabolic symptoms; others have reproductive or dermatological manifestations. It carries long-term risks of Type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and mental health conditions.

PCOS is not a single disease but a syndrome — the same root cause (hormonal dysregulation + insulin resistance + inflammation) manifests differently based on genetics, gut health, and lifestyle.

Root Causes

Insulin Resistance: Up to 70% of women with PCOS have insulin resistance. Elevated insulin stimulates ovaries to produce excess androgens, disrupting ovulation.

Chronic Low-Grade Inflammation: Higher inflammatory markers stimulate androgen production and worsen insulin resistance — creating a vicious cycle.

Gut Microbiome Dysbiosis: Research shows significantly different gut microbiome composition in PCOS — less diversity, altered bile acid metabolism, and increased intestinal permeability. This directly worsens hormonal and metabolic dysfunction.

Environmental Factors: Endocrine-disrupting chemicals (BPA, phthalates), chronic stress, poor sleep, and sedentary lifestyle all contribute to PCOS severity.

Anti-inflammatory nutrition: Low-glycemic, whole-food diet rich in colorful vegetables, legumes, healthy fats, and lean protein. Avoid refined carbohydrates, excess sugar, and ultra-processed foods.

Microbiome restoration: Fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi), prebiotic fiber (onions, garlic, oats), evidence-based probiotic supplementation to restore gut diversity.
03
Yoga & mindful movement: Both resistance training and aerobic exercise improve insulin sensitivity. Yoga — particularly pelvic-targeted practices — supports hormonal regulation and stress reduction.
04
Stress management: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which worsens insulin resistance and disrupts the HPO axis. Daily meditation, breathwork, and adequate sleep are non-negotiable.
05
Evidence-based supplements: Inositol (myo + D-chiro), magnesium, Vitamin D, zinc, and N-acetylcysteine have strong research support. Always discuss with your healthcare provider.

Hormonal, not just caloric — why standard diets fail women

Why Women Gain & Hold Weight Differently

Estrogen drives fat storage in hips, thighs, and abdomen. Post-menopausal women experience accelerated abdominal fat accumulation as estrogen declines. PCOS, thyroid dysfunction, and insulin resistance all compound this. The gut microbiome — now well-established as a regulator of body weight — shows distinctly different composition in obese women.

Up to 88% of overweight women have underlying insulin resistance. Without addressing this, sustainable fat loss is biologically near-impossible — not a matter of willpower.

Root Causes in Women

Hormonal Fat: Estrogen dominance, low progesterone, elevated cortisol (chronic stress), and declining estrogen at menopause all shift where and how women store fat.

Insulin Resistance: The most common metabolic driver. Cells stop responding to insulin; the pancreas overproduces it; fat storage is locked in and fat burning is blocked.

Gut Dysbiosis: Obese individuals have less gut microbial diversity and altered energy-extracting bacteria. The gut literally harvests more calories from food and increases fat-storage signaling.

Chronic Stress & Cortisol: Cortisol signals the body to store belly fat and break down muscle — the worst metabolic outcome. This creates a cycle that no diet alone can break.

Sleep Deprivation: A single night of poor sleep reduces insulin sensitivity by 25%. Chronically poor sleep makes weight loss physiologically very difficult.

01
Identify YOUR root driver: Is it hormonal, insulin-driven, gut-related, or stress-induced? A targeted assessment (blood tests, hormone panel, metabolic markers) reveals this — and determines the correct approach.
02
Low-glycemic, whole-food nutrition: Not a diet. An eating pattern that stabilises blood sugar, reduces insulin spikes, and supports gut health. Rich in fiber, protein, and healthy fats. Sustainable, not restrictive.
03
Post-meal walks + resistance training: A 10–15 minute walk after meals reduces post-meal blood glucose by 30–50%. Resistance training builds insulin-sensitive muscle. These two habits alone produce measurable results.
04
Gut microbiome restoration: 30+ plant species per week, fermented foods, adequate fiber. This addresses the gut driver of weight independently of calorie restriction.
05
Stress and sleep as medicine: Daily meditation, yoga, and 7–9 hours of consistent sleep are not optional lifestyle add-ons — they are direct metabolic interventions that affect weight more than most people realise.

Understanding PPD

PPD is distinct from "baby blues" (mild mood changes lasting 1–2 weeks) in its severity and duration. Estrogen and progesterone drop 90–95% in 24–48 hours after delivery — one of the most dramatic hormonal shifts the human body experiences. This crash profoundly affects serotonin and dopamine.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding dramatically deplete iron, omega-3 DHA, B12, iodine, folate, zinc, and Vitamin D — all critical for brain and mood function. The gut-brain axis is also severely disrupted during and after birth.

Root Causes

Hormonal Crash: The 90–95% drop in estrogen and progesterone after delivery directly impacts serotonin, dopamine, and GABA — the neurotransmitters that regulate mood and calm.

Nutritional Depletion: Pregnancy depletes iron, omega-3 DHA (critical for brain health), B12, iodine, folate, zinc, and Vitamin D. These deficiencies directly impair mood regulation.

Gut-Brain Axis Disruption: Birth, antibiotics, and the postpartum period severely alter the gut microbiome. Since 90% of serotonin is produced in the gut, dysbiosis directly contributes to depressive symptoms.

Sleep Deprivation: Chronic sleep deprivation alone can cause or mimic depression — new mothers face some of the most severe sleep disruption of their lives.

01
Seek professional support immediately: PPD is highly treatable. Psychotherapy (CBT, IPT) and when appropriate medication are first-line. Please reach out — there is no shame in this.
02
Nutritional replenishment: Iron-rich foods, omega-3 fatty acids, B vitamins, and Vitamin D. Targeted supplementation under guidance, especially if breastfeeding.
03
Restorative postnatal yoga: Gentle postnatal yoga supports physical recovery, reduces cortisol, and provides structured self-care. Even 15 minutes daily makes a measurable difference.
04
Meditation and mindfulness: Loving-kindness meditation particularly helps rebuild connection with your baby. Daily mindfulness, even briefly, is clinically shown to reduce PPD severity.

What is Gestational Diabetes?

GDM develops because pregnancy hormones (particularly human placental lactogen) cause insulin resistance in the mother. For most women, the pancreas compensates by producing more insulin. When it cannot compensate sufficiently, blood glucose rises — causing GDM.

Risk factors include: pre-pregnancy overweight/obesity, previous GDM, PCOS, family history of diabetes, age over 35, and certain ethnic backgrounds (South Asian women are at significantly higher risk).

Women with GDM have a 50% risk of developing Type 2 diabetes within 10 years. Their babies have elevated risk of childhood obesity and metabolic disease. Early, targeted intervention changes both trajectories.

Preconception Weight Optimisation

Achieving a healthy weight before conception significantly improves fertility, reduces risk of miscarriage, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, and delivery complications. Research shows even 5% weight loss in overweight women significantly improves ovulation and conception rates.

For women with PCOS — a leading cause of infertility — targeted weight management that addresses insulin resistance simultaneously treats both the weight AND the fertility challenge.

Recommended Weight Gain During Pregnancy

Guidelines vary by pre-pregnancy BMI: underweight women should gain 12–18 kg; normal weight 11–16 kg; overweight 7–11 kg; obese women 5–9 kg. These ranges support healthy fetal development while minimizing complications.

Our trimester-by-trimester nutrition plans ensure appropriate gain — with the right calories, protein, iron, calcium, omega-3s, and folate for each stage of development — while managing blood sugar and preventing excessive gain.

Postpartum Weight Recovery

The postpartum period demands compassion, not crash diets. Hormonal shifts, sleep deprivation, breastfeeding nutritional needs, and emotional changes make postpartum weight loss fundamentally different from other weight loss.

Breastfeeding burns approximately 500 extra calories per day — but cutting calories too aggressively reduces milk supply and depletes maternal nutrition reserves. The goal is recovery, restoration, and gradual weight loss over 6–12 months — not rapid loss.

The Autoimmune-Estrogen Connection

Estrogen modulates immune function — enhancing it in ways that protect against infection but also increase susceptibility to autoimmunity. This explains why autoimmune conditions cluster around hormonal transitions: puberty, pregnancy, postpartum, and menopause.

Common autoimmune conditions in women include: Hashimoto's thyroiditis (10% of women), Lupus SLE (9:1 female:male ratio), Rheumatoid Arthritis (3:1), Multiple Sclerosis (3:1), Sjögren's Syndrome (9:1), and Celiac Disease.

Dr. Raga Deepthi's personal journey through autoimmune challenges — combined with her PhD-level science — gives her uniquely deep insight into supporting women with these conditions.

Root Causes — The Triad

1. Gut Permeability ("Leaky Gut"): A compromised intestinal barrier allows bacterial components into the bloodstream, triggering immune activation. Dysbiosis consistently precedes and accompanies autoimmune flares. This is the most modifiable root cause.

2. Molecular Mimicry: Some gut bacteria and viruses share protein sequences with human tissues. The immune system, trained to attack the pathogen, mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues — this is how many autoimmune conditions are triggered.

3. Immune Dysregulation: Loss of immune tolerance — the ability to recognise "self" — is central to all autoimmune conditions. Chronic stress, sleep deprivation, nutrient deficiencies (especially Vitamin D), and environmental toxins all impair immune tolerance.

01
Heal the gut as priority one: An elimination diet (removing gluten, dairy, and other common triggers for 30–60 days), gut-healing foods (bone broth, collagen, L-glutamine), and microbiome restoration are foundational. Research consistently links gut health to autoimmune activity.
02
Anti-inflammatory nutrition: Mediterranean-style, phytonutrient-dense eating. Omega-3 fatty acids (fish, walnuts, flaxseed) reduce inflammatory cytokines that drive autoimmune flares. Avoid inflammatory ultra-processed foods, refined sugar, and seed oils.
03
Vitamin D optimisation: Vitamin D is one of the most powerful immune modulators. Deficiency is almost universal in autoimmune patients — and supplementation consistently reduces antibody levels and flare frequency. Target 60–80 ng/mL under medical supervision.
04
Stress reduction is non-negotiable: Cortisol dysregulation drives autoimmune flares. Daily meditation, yoga (especially restorative and yin styles), and sleep optimisation measurably reduce inflammatory markers and antibody levels.
05
Work alongside your specialist: Holistic approaches complement — not replace — rheumatology, endocrinology, or neurology care. The goal is to reduce disease activity, lower medication dosage over time, and improve quality of life.

The Thyroid-Gut-Hormone Connection

The thyroid regulates metabolism, temperature, heart rate, mood, and reproductive hormones. When it underperforms, everything slows — from digestion to cognition. Hashimoto's thyroiditis is an autoimmune condition where antibodies attack thyroid tissue, and is strongly linked to intestinal permeability (leaky gut).

Comprehensive thyroid testing should include TSH, Free T3, Free T4, Reverse T3, and TPO/thyroglobulin antibodies. Many women are told their thyroid is "normal" when only TSH is tested — missing subclinical dysfunction and active autoimmune disease.

Why Insulin Resistance Matters for Women

Chronically elevated insulin — the hallmark of insulin resistance — stimulates ovaries to produce excess androgens (directly causing PCOS), promotes estrogen dominance, drives fat storage (especially abdominal), and accelerates cellular aging. It is the metabolic driver underlying many of the most common conditions in women.

Standard fasting blood glucose can appear "normal" while insulin is already significantly elevated. Fasting insulin and HOMA-IR testing are far more sensitive and should be requested alongside blood glucose and HbA1c.

Why Menopause Demands Attention

Estrogen is not just a reproductive hormone — it protects the heart, bones, brain, urinary tract, and skin. Its decline at menopause increases risk for cardiovascular disease (the leading cause of death in women), osteoporosis, dementia, and metabolic syndrome.
